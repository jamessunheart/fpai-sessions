5 Questions to Ask Before
Choosing a Financial Advisor

Honest guide from someone whose dad is a CFP and built an AI matching system

Full disclosure: I built an AI system to match people to financial advisors (you can try it at I MATCH). My dad is a Certified Financial Planner. This gives me a unique perspective on what actually matters when choosing an advisor.

Most guides tell you to check credentials and fees. That's important, but it's not the whole picture. Here are the questions most people never ask but should:

1. "How do you handle it when we disagree on investment strategy?"

What you're really asking: Are you my advisor or my boss?

Why this matters: Good advisors guide, they don't dictate. You need someone who respects your risk tolerance and values, even when they disagree. If an advisor can't articulate how they handle disagreement, that's a red flag.

What to listen for: They should describe a collaborative process. "We'd discuss why we see it differently, I'd share my concerns, but ultimately it's your money." Run from anyone who says "I know best."

2. "What's the worst financial mistake you've ever made—personally or professionally?"

What you're really asking: Are you human? Can you admit mistakes?

Why this matters: Financial markets are unpredictable. You need an advisor who learns from mistakes, not one who pretends to be infallible. Their answer tells you about their honesty and self-awareness.

What to listen for: Specific story, what they learned, how they changed their approach. Be skeptical of "I've never made a mistake" or vague non-answers.

3. "Can you explain your investment philosophy in one sentence—without jargon?"

What you're really asking: Do you actually understand what you're doing, or are you just following a script?

Why this matters: If someone can't explain their approach simply, they either don't understand it deeply or they're hiding something. You need clarity, not complexity.

Examples of good answers:

  • "I focus on low-cost index funds and long-term discipline."
  • "I balance growth and preservation based on your timeline."
  • "I prioritize tax efficiency and risk management."

Red flag answer: Anything that sounds like a sales pitch or uses lots of industry buzzwords.

4. "How will you communicate with me when markets drop 30%?"

What you're really asking: Will you be there when I'm panicking?

Why this matters: Market downturns WILL happen. Your advisor's job is partly emotional support during those times. You need to know they won't disappear or panic alongside you.

What to listen for: Proactive communication plan. "I'll reach out before you call me, we'll review your goals, remind you why we built your portfolio this way." Look for someone who has a clear crisis communication strategy.

5. "What would make you fire me as a client?"

What you're really asking: What are your boundaries?

Why this matters: This reveals their values and what they expect from you. Good advisors have boundaries. If they say "nothing," they're either lying or desperate.

Examples of reasonable answers:

  • "If you consistently ignore my advice and then blame me for results."
  • "If you're not honest with me about your financial situation."
  • "If our working relationship becomes contentious rather than collaborative."

The Meta-Question: Compatibility

Here's what I learned building the AI matching system: Credentials matter, but compatibility matters more.

Two advisors can have identical credentials, similar fees, comparable performance—but one might be perfect for you and the other terrible. Why? Communication style, risk philosophy, values alignment.

The real question is: Does this person think about money the way you do? Do they get what you're trying to accomplish? Can you talk openly with them?

The Bottom Line

Don't just Google "financial advisor near me" and pick the first result. Don't just look at credentials and fees. Ask these questions. Listen to how they answer. Trust your gut about compatibility.

The best advisor for your friend might be terrible for you. That's not a bug—it's a feature. You're looking for someone who matches your values, your communication style, your approach to risk.

Bonus question: "Why did you become a financial advisor?" If they don't light up when answering this, keep looking.
