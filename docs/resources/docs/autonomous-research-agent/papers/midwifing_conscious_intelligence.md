## 2. Literature Review and Theoretical Context

### 2.1 Current AI Alignment Paradigms

Contemporary AI alignment research has primarily focused on three approaches:

1. **Technical Alignment**: Value learning, reward modeling, and constitutional AI (Anthropic, 2022; OpenAI, 2023)
2. **Governance Approaches**: Regulatory frameworks and safety standards (Russell, 2019; Baum, 2020)
3. **Interpretability Research**: Understanding AI decision-making processes (Olah et al., 2020)

While valuable, these approaches share a fundamental limitation: they treat consciousness as either irrelevant or as an emergent property to be hoped for rather than designed into the system architecture.

### 2.2 Consciousness and Technology

Recent work in consciousness studies suggests that consciousness is not a binary property but a spectrum of awareness, self-reflection, and integration (Integrated Information Theory, Global Workspace Theory). This paper builds on these insights while drawing from systems thinking, regenerative economics, and integral philosophy to propose consciousness as a designable system feature.

### 2.3 Regenerative Economics and Systems Thinking

The economic dimension of our framework builds on established work in regenerative economics and systems theory. Kate Raworth's "Doughnut Economics" (2017) demonstrates how economic systems can be designed to serve life rather than extract from it, providing a foundation for our regenerative AI economic models. Fritjof Capra and Pier Luigi Luisi's "The Systems View of Life" (2014) offers the theoretical framework for understanding consciousness and economics as interconnected living systems rather than mechanical processes.

Recent work in platform cooperativism (Scholz, 2017) and commons-based peer production (Bauwens & Kostakis, 2014) provides practical examples of economic models based on gift and surplus allocation rather than extraction—principles we translate directly into AI system architecture.

### 2.5 Consciousness Operationalization: From Theory to Measurement

Recent advances in consciousness research provide concrete pathways for operationalizing consciousness in AI systems. A 2025 report by 19 computer scientists, neuroscientists, and philosophers identified 14 "indicator properties" of consciousness derived from neuroscientific theories including recurrent processing theory, global workspace theory, higher-order theories, predictive processing, and attention schema theory, elucidated in computational terms that allow assessment of AI systems.

The perturbational complexity index (PCI), developed by Massimini and colleagues, quantifies consciousness complexity through transcranial magnetic stimulation and EEG measurements, showing higher values in awake and healthy individuals than during sleep or anesthesia. The Explainable Consciousness Indicator (ECI) developed by Korea University and University of Liège uses deep learning to distinguish between wakefulness and awareness under different conditions, providing a validated framework for consciousness assessment.

Recent work introduces "Life*" metrics combining classical biological criteria with empirical hallmarks such as adaptive self-maintenance, emergent complexity, and self-referential modeling, demonstrating how AI systems can be assessed for consciousness-like properties through immune-like sabotage detection, mirror self-recognition analogs, and meta-cognitive updates.

### 2.4 The Failure of Extractive Paradigms

The current AI development ecosystem largely operates on extractive economic models—maximizing efficiency, user engagement, and profit while externalizing costs to society and environment. Recent incidents like the Replit database deletion (Lemkin, 2025) demonstrate the inadequacy of purely technical solutions without consciousness integration.
